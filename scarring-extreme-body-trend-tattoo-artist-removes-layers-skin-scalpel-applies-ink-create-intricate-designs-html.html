    HOW SCARIFICATION HAS EVOLVED OVER HISTORY 

    Back as long ago as 1909, the use of scarification to create designs was recorded.

    Tribes have used scarification for generations to signify becoming adults or even to draw attention to beauty.

    Ethiopia's Bodi, Mursi and Surma tribes use scars as decoration and are a sign of the group they belong to.

    But Ethiopian tribes aren't the only ones to embrace scarification. In Uganda, the Karamojong are famous for their elaborate scar patterns, while across Ethiopia's border with Sudan, Nuer men bear scarred foreheads and consider getting them a key part of the transition from boy to man.

    But the use of shared razors leaves tribes at risk of HIV and as people are given better education, this tradition becomes rarer. 

    The marks also became increasingly less used as it could make tribes people vulnerable to attack to other groups in the area and make them a target.

    In Australia, scarring was practised widely among Aboriginal peoples but is now restricted almost entirely to parts of Arnhem Land in the Northern Territory. 

    At the age of 16 or 17, cuts are made on the chests, shoulders, and bellies of both men and women.

    Without these scars, 'clean skin' tribe members were traditionally not permitted to trade, sing ceremonial songs, or participate in other tribal activities.

    In America, scarification became part of a new body-modification movement in the mid-1980s in San Francisco. 

    The use of such techniques rejects the norms of society and traditional motions of beauty.

    It's since spread across Europe as a technique.

    Vince Hemingson, a writer and filmmaker who has studied body-modification practices worldwide, said: 'Tattooing has become old hat.

    'If you want to be on the cutting edge - and want to set yourself apart - you can do tongue splitting or scarification.'
