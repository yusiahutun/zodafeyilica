    Harris Reed on embracing peacocking, finding your personal style and the transformative power of jew

    Harris Reed has the sort of energy you wish you could bottle up and save for a rainy day. After just a few minutes chatting on Zoom, there's already a remarkable feeling of familiarity - as if I've known the designer for years. The 25-year-old is both interesting and interested, talking with an infectious confidence and enthusiasm - their trademark red hair and copious amounts of jewellery flashing across the screen as they gesticulate. That Reed is decked out in jewels is no surprise, given they have just launched headfirst into the world of jewellery for the very first time.

    “I love jewellery in the same way that I love make-up,” Harris tells me. “In a second I can take all this off, put it in my bag and then layer it all back up again. Jewellery has that power to transform.”

    Much like everything the designer turns their hand to, a new collaboration with fashion’s favourite jewellery brand Missoma came about in the most organic way, much like Reed's previous partnerships with artists such as Harry Styles and Solange.

    Jewellery is perfect for experimenting and is the best way to play up your identity

    “I was at a dinner one night and the CEO and founder of Missoma was there [Marisa Hordern] and we talked for multiple hours about our love [for] and obsession with jewellery and beauty,” Reed tells me. “We almost made the deal on the spot. She asked me, ‘Do you want to do jewellery together?' And I said, 'Fuck yeah.'”

    One thing that made the partnership a no-brainer for Reed was Missoma’s reputation. “I obviously knew of Missoma because it’s such a staple in people’s lives; everyone has a Missoma chain so I knew that the quality was there,” they explain. “Marisa really gets what I want to do.”

    Courtesy of Missoma

    Reed's love for jewellery goes back as early as learning to walk and talk. “I was always obsessed with being adorned in flea-market finds, as well as my grandmother and mother’s jewellery. I could barely crunch my fingers over the sewing machine I was wearing so many rings.”

    In fact, it's those very pieces which formed the inspiration for this new collection. “I have worn this ring on my finger since I was four,” they tell me, doing a show-and-tell style presentation of their jewels. “This is from a flea market in Paris, it’s kind of falling apart and has missing pieces, but throughout my designs I reference the intricacy, detailing and labradorite stone. It happened subconsciously, I didn’t even realise.”

    “I always wear this thin bracelet from my grandmother,” they say, shaking some seriously stacked wrists. “I’ve moved 30 times, left America and came to the UK to really start something for myself. This is why I love jewellery, as you can always have those ornate and beautiful pieces with you forever, wherever you go.”

    Courtesy of Missoma

    Reed’s design aesthetic is best described as fluid romanticism with a modern twist. And, when it comes to jewellery, there is no such thing as too much; layering is very much encouraged and all the pieces are designed to be mixed and matched together. This is maximalism at its finest.

    If you feel good then you’re doing it right, no matter what anyone else says

    “Jewellery is perfect for experimenting and is the best way to play up your identity. You can have fun with it and see what’s comfortable for you. If you’re in a situation where it feels too much, you can easily take a piece off on the tube and put it back on when you arrive wherever you’re headed,” they laugh. “It’s a really easy, fluid way of playing with your identity, how you want to express and peacock yourself to the world.”

    That's the message that underpins everything Reed does: it doesn’t matter who you are or what your style is, as long as you are being your most authentic self. “I believe you have to dress for yourself first and then others take notice,” they tell me. “There’s nothing better than getting dressed and feeling that you look good, then heading out and having everyone smile at you. They appreciate you being the most authentic to yourself. If we dress up for other people, then we are likely seeking approval or looking for something.”

    Courtesy of Missoma

    And don't even mention the idea of following any kind of fashion rulebook. “I’ve always piled jewellery on because that felt like me, but if that doesn’t feel like you then that’s OK. Layer it in your way or wear one piece as a statement; it’s very much 'do it for you'. If you feel good then you’re doing it right, no matter what anyone else says.”

    Of course, dressing exactly how you want to (no matter how bold that may be), can be a daunting prospect. To leave the house with no reservations about being out of your comfort zone is much easier said than done, so how does one exude total confidence when you’re feeling the opposite?

    “My favourite thing to do is get dressed up with friends just at home having dinner and cocktails,” laughs Reed. “Make sure you do it in a safe space where you feel comfortable. You will make lots of ‘mistakes’ in terms of things not feeling right and that’s OK. It’s a lot to go out of your comfort zone so doing it with friends helps.”

    But even those as flamboyant as Reed sometimes feel the need to tone it down. The designer spent lockdown dressing up in clothes bought at flea markets and taking polaroids of themselves at home, when stepping out in head-to-toe sequins didn't feel quite appropriate. “Even for me as someone who dresses up all the time, it felt like too much, so it was a very comfortable way for me to walk around doing housework, dripping in my grandmother’s pearls and wearing an oversized faux fur coat,” they laugh. “It’s important to feel comfortable in intimate surroundings with friends or alone - it makes jumping on the tube, popping to Tescos or out for a fabulous night far less daunting.”

    It’s about giving zero fucks to what anyone says and not caring what people think about me

    As was the case for many, the pandemic allowed Reed to slow down and reflect, the results of which are evident in the care taken with the Missoma collection. Harris designed and created the line during lockdown, meaning there was a welcomed shift in pace. “I had so much time when normally things would be rushed, so I had more time for the collection to breathe and to consider things like packaging and intricate details,” they explain. “I hope people will keep this mindfulness and examine what they’re buying instead of clicking on everything. Before the pandemic, everything was fast-paced, crazy, trend-led and [everyone was] dressing like everyone else. It’s been a big reset.”

    So what change does Reed want to see from consumers and the industry, as a result of the events of the last 18 months? “We need to focus on buying less and instead investing on what’s right for you rather than feeling like you have to be someone else. People are being more authentic to themselves as they have the time to take to realise what they want.”

    Courtesy of Missoma

    They pause. “For me personally, it's about giving zero fucks to what anyone says and not caring what people think about me and my body. We’ve been stuck at home reflecting and coming into who we are so I think that is continuing forward. After nearly two years of the pandemic, we are all craving a little bit of decadence and inspiration.

    “I’m living my truth, walking down the street in my platforms covered in jewellery, knowing I’m making change and a safer space for everyone to come out and be who they want to be."
